1. Background

Gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM) is a form of diabetes that develops during pregnancy and affects both maternal and fetal health. It is associated with increased risks of complications such as preeclampsia, cesarean delivery, macrosomia, neonatal hypoglycemia, and a higher likelihood of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.

Current screening for GDM commonly occurs between 24 and 28 weeks of gestation using an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT). While effective, this timing may delay opportunities for earlier monitoring and preventive interventions in patients at elevated risk.

Machine learning offers the potential to estimate a patient's risk earlier in pregnancy using routinely collected clinical information, allowing healthcare providers to identify higher-risk patients sooner.

2. Problem Statement

Can gestational diabetes be accurately predicted during early pregnancy using routinely collected maternal characteristics?

3. Business (Clinical) Objective

Develop an explainable machine learning model that estimates a pregnant patient's risk of developing gestational diabetes using information that would typically be available during the early stages of prenatal care.

The goal is not to diagnose GDM, but to support clinicians in identifying patients who may benefit from additional monitoring or preventive interventions.

10. Key Risks
Data Leakage

This is the biggest technical risk.

Features such as OGTT may have been collected during diagnostic testing rather than early pregnancy.

If so, including them in the primary model could leak information about the outcome and overstate model performance.

We'll investigate the timing of each variable and document our rationale for including or excluding them.

Missing Data

Several clinical variables have missing values.

We'll explore:

amount of missingness,
patterns of missingness,
and appropriate imputation strategies.
Generalizability

The dataset may represent a single population or healthcare setting.

We'll discuss how this could affect performance in other populations.

Bias

We'll examine whether model performance differs across available patient subgroups and discuss potential sources of bias without over-interpreting the findings.
